Bullying

Bullying is a widely known topic that students are exposed to experience in school.

What is bullying?

According to the Centers for Disease Control and Department of Education:

• unwanted, aggressive behavior among school aged children that involves a real or perceived power imbalance.



There are different kinds of bullying:

➝ Verbal bullying:

• Teasing

• Name calling

• Innappropriate sexual comments

• Threats

➝ Social bullying:

• Leaving someone out on purpose

• Telling people not to be friends with someone

• Spreading rumors

• Embarrassing someone

➝ Physical bullying:

• Hitting

• Spitting

• Tripping/pushing

• Mean/rude hand gestures

Political Terminology

A brief index of often used political terms.

***Democrat: A member of the Democratic political party or someone who believes in equality for all people and ruling by the majority.

***Republican: Relates to a major political party in the United States that is associated with business interests and favors a limited government role in economic matters

• Democracy: A system of government in which people share the power. The United States is a democracy.

• Communism: A system of government in which classes don't exist, and the government owns all property.

• Delegates: A representative to a party's national convention chosen by local voters to vote for a particular candidate.

• Dictatorship: A government that is controlled by one person.

• Anarchy: A social structure that is without a formal form of government.

• Convention: A national meeting of a political party where the delegates elect a nominee.

• GOP: Grand Old Party, nickname for the republican party.

• Left-wing: Liberal. The labeling system originated from the seating pattern of the French National Assembly, which put liberals on the left, moderates in the middle, and conservatives on the right.

• Poll: A survey used to gauge public opinion concerning issues or to forecast an election.

• Primary: A state election in which party members vote for a candidate from within their party. The vote determines how many of that state's delegates each candidate gets.

• Nominee: The candidate chosen by a political party to run for a particular office.

• Silent Majority: The mass of Americans whose opinions are not loud and public, but who together have enormous power.

• Swing Vote: The undecided, usually independent, portion of the electorate that can "swing" the outcome of an election one way or the other.

Voting?

So, you've probably wondered how to vote once or twice. Here's how.

First, you have to register.

To register, simply print out the voter registration form that corresponds to your state, and either mail it to your state's Board of Elections Office.

OR

If you rather do it in person, it's pretty much the exact same process, the only difference is that instead of mailing your form, you would hand deliver it to your local Elections Office.


But wait, now what?


Now you vote.

If you're younger than eighteen, then you would of course have wait until you become of age. But if you are eighteen, then go ahead and vote!

To vote however, you must have a valid and current form of identification (student I.D, drivers license, passport, etc.)and request an vote-by-ballot online, which you can literally find by looking up as "vote by ballot online" into Google search. But, if you would rather vote in person, simply look up "voting locations"(usually at libraries) and just go to one of the locations given!



DON'T FORGET TO RESEARCH THE VOTING DATES!

The dates listed are the only days that you can actually vote.
